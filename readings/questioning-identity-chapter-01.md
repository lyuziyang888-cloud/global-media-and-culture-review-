# Questions of Identity

## Bibliographic Details

Kath Woodward, "Questions of Identity," Chapter 1 in *Questioning Identity: Gender, Class, Ethnicity*, pp. 5-41.

## Scope Note

The supplied PDF contains the full 171-page book. This reading map covers Chapter 1 only. Chapters 2-4 should not be treated as Unit 2 material unless the course specifies them.

## Central Argument

Identity links the personal and the social. It is formed through identification, similarity and difference, symbols, representation and institutions, while reflecting an ongoing tension between social structure and human agency.

## Core Questions

1. How are identities formed?
2. How much control do people have in shaping their identities?
3. Why can social change produce both uncertainty and new identity possibilities?

## Key Arguments

- Identity is not the same as personality; it involves active identification.
- Identity connects self-understanding with recognition by others.
- People hold multiple, potentially conflicting and changing identities.
- Symbols and representations communicate identity and mark difference.
- Institutions classify people and make some identities possible while restricting others.
- Individuals and collective movements can negotiate, resist and reconstruct identities, but never without limits.
