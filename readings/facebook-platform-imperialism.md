# Facebook's Platform Imperialism

## Bibliographic Details

Dal Yong Jin, "Facebook's Platform Imperialism: The Economics and Geopolitics of Social Media," in *Media Imperialism: Continuity and Change* (2019), pp. 187-198.

## Central Argument

Facebook is a platform capitalist and a dominant platform imperialist whose global expansion reinforces asymmetrical economic, political and cultural power between the United States and non-Western countries.

## Argument Map

```text
Privately owned platform infrastructure
-> collection and commodification of user data
-> advertising revenue and network effects
-> global market dominance
-> unequal international capital and data flows
-> support from and usefulness to US state power
```

## Key Evidence

- Platforms are corporate mediators, not neutral intermediaries.
- Facebook monetises aggregated user attention and data through targeted advertising.
- Network effects help Facebook become a default platform and displace local rivals.
- Jin argues that Facebook's international users and revenue produce unequal global flows.
- Lobbying, trade policy, political targeting and surveillance connect platform power to state power.

## Limits And Cautions

- The chapter was published in 2019 and uses data mainly from 2017-2018; figures must not be presented as current.
- Facebook is the main case, so broader claims about all platforms require additional evidence.
- Non-US platforms complicate a simple US-versus-rest model, although Jin argues they had not balanced the global order.

## Possible Exam Questions

- How does platform imperialism extend media imperialism?
- Why are digital platforms not neutral infrastructures?
- Explain the relationship between platform capitalism and data commodification.
