# Facebook as a Platform Imperialist

## Case Type

Platform capitalism / platform imperialism / data commodification / state-corporate power

## What This Case Shows

Jin uses Facebook to argue that digital platforms renew US media imperialism through global market dominance, data extraction, advertising, network effects and support from state power.

## Evidence Chain

1. Facebook mediates social activity on privately owned infrastructure.
2. User activity and data support targeted advertising and profit.
3. Network effects strengthen Facebook against local competitors such as Korea's Cyworld.
4. Revenue increasingly comes from users and markets outside the United States.
5. Lobbying and US policy support global market expansion.
6. Platform data can be connected to political targeting and state surveillance.

## Evaluation

The case strongly exposes asymmetrical ownership and data flows. However, the chapter also acknowledges non-US platforms, especially Chinese platforms, as potential challengers even though Jin argues they had not produced a balanced global order.

## Exam Sentence

Facebook demonstrates that global connectivity can coexist with highly concentrated ownership, data extraction and geopolitical power.

## Source Anchor

Jin, "Facebook's Platform Imperialism," pp. 187-198. Historical figures in the chapter should be cited as evidence from 2018, not as current statistics.
